# Fiscal Capacity & Tax Structure in Africa (2000–2024)
An end-to-end data analysis project exploring fiscal capacity and tax structure trends across African countries (2000–2024), using Excel dashboards and macro-fiscal data.

# Tools & Skills

- Excel (Dashboards, Data Cleaning, Pivot Tables)
- Data Analysis
- Economic & Fiscal Analysis
- Data Visualization
- Storytelling with Data

  

# Project Overview

This project analyzes the evolution of tax structures across African countries from 2000 to 2024, using macro-fiscal data expressed as a percentage of GDP.

The objective is to understand how governments generate revenue, identify structural patterns, and highlight opportunities to improve fiscal capacity.

The analysis is presented through interactive dashboards and supported by key insights and policy-oriented interpretations.

# Dataset Information

--Dataset Name
IMF World Revenue Longitudinal Database (WoRLD) – 2026 Release

Link: https://www.imf.org/en/topics/fiscal-policies/world-revenue-longitudinal-database

-Description

The World Revenue Longitudinal Database (WoRLD) is a comprehensive dataset developed by the IMF that tracks government revenue trends from the early 1980s to the present.

It includes data for 195 countries and provides consistent, comparable indicators on:

Total revenue
Tax revenue composition
Non-tax revenue sources
GDP (in local currency)

The dataset is updated annually and is widely used for:

Fiscal policy analysis
Cross-country comparisons
Revenue mobilization studies


-Key Variables Used in This Project
TotRev → Total revenue (% of GDP)
TaxRev → Total tax revenue (% of GDP)
TaxInc → Income taxes (% of GDP)
TaxIncC → Corporate income taxes (% of GDP)
TaxIncCRes → Extractive industry taxes (% of GDP)
TaxPro → Property taxes (% of GDP)
TaxSal → Sales and production taxes (% of GDP)
TaxTra → Trade taxes (% of GDP)
NonTaxRes → Non-tax revenue from natural resources (% of GDP)

Key Insights

Consumption Taxes (Sales & Production)
Show a clear and steady upward trend (≈4.0% → >5.5%).
→ Indicates increasing reliance on indirect taxation (e.g., VAT), which is easier to collect and more stable.

Income Taxes
More volatile, with moderate growth until ~2012 and stabilization afterward.
→ Reflect sensitivity to economic cycles such as employment and income fluctuations.

Property Taxes
Remain very low and unstable, with peaks around 2010 and 2017.
→ Suggest structural limitations such as weak cadastral systems and low administrative capacity.

COVID-19 Impact (2020)
A visible drop across all tax categories reflects economic disruption.

Conclusion

African tax systems are gradually shifting toward consumption-based taxation, while direct taxes remain underdeveloped and less stable.

-Sales and Production Taxes

Increase steadily (≈4.0 → 5.6% of GDP)

Strength: stable and predictable revenue
Risk: regressive impact on lower-income populations

- Taxes on International Trade

Decline over time (≈3.3 → 2.4% of GDP)

 Reflect trade liberalization
 Shift toward domestic taxation (VAT-based systems)

- Property Taxes

Remain extremely low (~0.15–0.22% of GDP)

  High untapped potential
  Weak implementation and administrative challenges

- Natural Resource Revenues
Non-tax revenues dominate
Tax revenues from extractive industries are relatively smaller

  Strong reliance on resource rents
  High exposure to commodity price volatility

Key Insight from This Dashboard

There is high heterogeneity across countries:

Some rely on natural resources
Others on consumption taxes
Few have strong balanced tax systems

High corporate tax shares may reflect narrow tax bases rather than strong fiscal capacity

→This reinforces that Africa cannot be treated as a single fiscal unit

4. Overall Conclusion

African countries show a structural pattern of:

Heavy reliance on consumption taxes (stable but regressive)
Dependence on non-tax resource revenues (volatile)

Meanwhile:

Income taxes are inconsistent
Property taxes are largely underdeveloped

→ This limits fiscal resilience and revenue diversification.

🚀 5. Policy Recommendations

Develop Property Taxation

Reduce Resource Dependence

Diversify revenue sources

Enhance Tax Administration

Invest in digital systems

- Limitations
Aggregates Africa as a whole → hides country-level differences
Does not include full fiscal indicators:
Total revenue (~20.8% of GDP)
Tax revenue (~12.8% of GDP)
No causal inference (descriptive analysis only)

- Executive Summary

The analysis reveals a structural shift toward consumption-based taxation across African countries. While these taxes provide stable revenue, they may increase inequality. At the same time, income and property taxes remain underdeveloped, and natural resource revenues introduce volatility. Strengthening direct taxation and diversifying revenue sources are key to improving fiscal capacity.
